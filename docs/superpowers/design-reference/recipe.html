Uncle Sal's Bet-Winning Chili

"The Chili That Paid for Sal's Honda."

Hardware

  • 2 lbs Ground Chuck (80/20)
  • 1 large Yellow Onion, chopped
  • 3 cloves Garlic, smashed
  • 2 cans (15oz) Kidney Beans
  • 1 can (28oz) Crushed Tomatoes
  • 1/4 cup Chili Powder (Don't skimp)
  • 1 tbsp Cumin
  • 1 tsp Black Pepper
  • Salt to taste
  • 1/2 cup Black Coffee (Secret)

Execution

  1. Brown the meat. Get a heavy pot. Heat it. Throw in the beef. Don't touch it until crust forms.
  2. Sweat the veg. Toss in onions and garlic. Cook till translucent.
  3. Spice dump. Dump in chili powder, cumin, pepper. Stir constantly for 1 minute till it smells like trouble.
  4. Liquid phase. Pour in tomatoes, beans (undrained), and the black coffee. Scrape the bottom.
  5. The wait. Simmer low and slow. 2 hours minimum. If you touch it, Sal will know.
The cast-iron pot Sal swears by (and won't let you touch).

The Story

It was August 1984. The annual Saint Anthony's parish cook-off was usually a polite affair, dominated by mild casseroles and overcooked ziti. Then Uncle Sal pulled up in his rust-bucket station wagon, hauling a cast-iron cauldron that looked like it belonged to a medieval warlock.

He didn't bring decorations for his table. He just brought a ladle and a scowl. The chili was thick, aggressive, and dark as motor oil—thanks to the secret addition of yesterday's black coffee. He didn't offer sour cream. He didn't offer cheese. If you asked for crackers, he stared at you until you walked away.

The judges were terrified. The priest coughed nervously after his first bite. But the depth of flavor—the smoky, beefy, unapologetic heat—was undeniable. He took first place through sheer intimidation and a recipe that commanded respect. The prize money was fifty bucks. He used it to put gas in the Honda. He's been bragging about it ever since.
